## What Is Fair Play?

A household management system by Eve Rodsky. 100 task cards representing everything it takes to run a home and family. It's a card game couples play together — but the real goal is eliminating invisible labor imbalances, not "winning."

The system came from interviews with 500+ couples and addresses the fact that in most households, one partner (usually the woman) carries most of the mental/cognitive load even when physical tasks are "split."

---

## The 4 Rules

### Rule 1: All Time Is Created Equal
Both partners' time has equal value regardless of income, job title, or how time is spent. No one's hour is worth more.

### Rule 2: Start Where You Are Now
Be honest about current state. No blame for past. Assess who actually holds what cards today.

### Rule 3: Establish Your Values and Standards
Discuss WHY certain tasks matter and agree on standards. What does "clean kitchen" mean to each of you? Define MSC (Minimum Standard of Care) together.

### Rule 4: Reclaim Your Right to Be Interesting
Each person deserves Unicorn Space — time for personal passions and growth. Your identity shouldn't be limited to your responsibilities.

## CPE: Conception, Planning, Execution

The core insight of Fair Play. Every household task has THREE invisible phases:

### Conception (C)
Noticing the task needs doing. "The milk is low." "Winter coats season is coming." "Dentist appointment is overdue." This is the *awareness* and *mental monitoring* phase.

### Planning (P)
Figuring out logistics. "When are we free? Who needs to be told? Do we need supplies? What's the budget?" This is the *cognitive labor* phase.

### Execution (E)
Actually doing the thing. Shopping, scheduling, showing up, cooking, cleaning.

**Key insight**: The deepest resentment lives in C and P — that's where the bulk of mental and emotional weight sits. Most "help" from a partner is just E (execution) while leaving C and P on the other person. Fair Play says: **when you hold a card, you own ALL THREE phases.**

This eliminates "RAT" (Random Assignment of Tasks) — the pattern where one partner mentally manages everything and delegates only execution ("Can you pick up milk?"), retaining all the cognitive load.

---

## MSC: Minimum Standard of Care

The card holder completes their task to a mutually agreed standard:
- **Without reminders**
- **Without a to-do list from their partner**
- **Without micromanaging**
- **Without the other partner checking in**

Partners must define what "good enough" looks like for each task together. Prevents perfectionism traps and micromanagement cycles.

## The 5 Card Suits

| Suit | Description | Approx Count |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| **Home** | Household maintenance — cleaning, dishes, laundry, dry cleaning, home repairs | ~20 |
| **Out** | External errands — auto, bills, calendar keeping, electronics/IT, civic engagement | ~15 |
| **Caregiving** | Child/dependent care — bathing, bedtime, clothes, dental, medical, school | ~25 |
| **Magic** | Relationship & atmosphere — birthdays, holidays, friendships, discipline, extended family | ~15 |
| **Wild** | Life events & flex — death, aging parents, moving, pets, special needs | ~15 |

Additional special cards:
- **Unicorn Space** (Player 1 & Player 2) — personal passion time
- **Daily Grinds** — recurring mundane tasks that can't be scheduled flexibly (dishes, trash, meals)
- **The Happiness Trio** — self-care, personal friendships, unicorn space

The deck has 60 cards for general households + 40 additional for families with children. Not all 100 apply to every family — you select only the relevant ones.

## How Couples Actually Play

### Initial Deal
1. Both partners sit together during calm, dedicated time
2. Review all 100 cards — remove irrelevant ones
3. Discuss each card: who currently handles it? Who should?
4. Assign clear ownership — ONE person owns each card completely (all CPE)
5. Define MSC together for each card
6. Document the deal
7. Commit — no random task assignment after this

### Day-to-Day
- Card holders own full CPE — no reminders needed
- The non-holder trusts the holder to handle it to MSC
- No micromanaging or checking in
- If something falls through, it's a conversation about the card, not a blame game

### Re-Dealing
Cards get re-dealt when circumstances change:
- Job changes
- New baby or family changes
- Health changes
- Kids' new activities
- Aging parent care needs
- General unsustainability

Regular check-ins assess what's working. It's a living system, not a one-time negotiation.

## What This Means for Our App

### Current app vs. Fair Play methodology gaps:

1. **Our categories don't match**: We use `daily-grind`, `kids`, `home`, `magic`, `wild`. Fair Play uses `Home`, `Out`, `Caregiving`, `Magic`, `Wild` + `Unicorn Space` + `Daily Grinds` as a cross-cutting tag.

2. **No CPE tracking**: Our app treats cards as simple tasks with a "completed" checkbox. Fair Play cards are about **ownership** — who holds the card, meaning who is responsible for all of C, P, and E. There's no "daily completion" — you hold the card or you don't.

3. **Completion doesn't make sense the way we have it**: A Fair Play card like "Meals (Weekday)" isn't a task you "check off." You HOLD it — meaning you own the conception, planning, and execution of weekday meals indefinitely until a re-deal. The checkmark UX is misleading.

4. **Missing MSC concept**: Partners should be able to discuss and record what the minimum standard is for each card.

5. **No Unicorn Space**: This is a critical part of Fair Play — both partners need protected personal time.

6. **Re-dealing is the key interaction**: The main app flow should be: select relevant cards → deal them → live with the deal → re-deal when needed. Not daily check-ins.
